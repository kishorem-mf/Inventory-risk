## 1. Introduction

In today‚Äôs global economy, supply chains are increasingly complex, spanning multiple geographies, partners, and markets. Companies must simultaneously manage:

* **High customer service expectations** (fast delivery, high availability).
* **Cost efficiency pressures** (inventory reduction, optimized transport).
* **Disruption risks** (supplier delays, geopolitical issues, pandemics).

To remain competitive, enterprises are shifting from **reactive, siloed supply chain management** to **integrated, predictive, and adaptive planning platforms**. These platforms enable real-time visibility, scenario simulation, and collaborative decision-making.

---

## 2. Supply Chain Network Overview

A modern supply chain is a **multi-tier network** consisting of:

1. **Suppliers (Tier 1, Tier 2)**

   * Provide raw materials, subassemblies, or finished goods.
   * Orders influenced by *lead times, minimum order quantities (MOQ), capacity, and reliability*.

2. **Manufacturing Plants**

   * Convert raw materials into finished products.
   * Governed by *production capacity, lot sizing rules, cycle times, and workforce availability*.

3. **Regional Distribution Centers (RDCs)**

   * Serve as consolidation points for finished goods.
   * Balance **supply push from plants** with **demand pull from markets**.

4. **Local Distribution Centers (DCs)**

   * Closer to retailers, wholesalers, and direct-to-consumer channels.
   * Handle **shorter planning horizons** and **volatile demand swings**.

5. **End Customers**

   * The ultimate demand driver.
   * Can be businesses (B2B) or individuals (B2C).

Each node is interlinked by **transportation flows**, influenced by *lead times, costs, and carrier constraints*.

---

## 3. Core Planning Key Figures

The supply chain‚Äôs efficiency depends on a set of **key figures** that capture demand, supply, and inventory positions:

* **Dependent Demand** ‚Üí Future demand requirements (forecast + confirmed orders).
* **Stock on Hand** ‚Üí Current inventory available at a location.
* **Projected Stock** ‚Üí Anticipated stock = *(Stock on Hand + Inbound Supply ‚Äì Outbound Demand)*.
* **Total Open PO/STO** ‚Üí Firmed supply already committed in the system.
* **Incoming Transport Receipts** ‚Üí Shipments in transit within lead times.
* **Outgoing Supply** ‚Üí Planned shipments to downstream nodes.
* **Safety Stock** ‚Üí Minimum buffer to cover variability.
* **Inventory Target** ‚Üí Desired inventory aligned to service/cost trade-offs.

These figures provide the **numerical foundation** for monitoring and decision-making.

## 4. Planning Horizons

Supply chain planning typically operates across three horizons:

* **Strategic (12‚Äì24 months)**

  * Network design, supplier strategy, capacity expansions.

* **Tactical (3‚Äì12 months)**

  * Demand-supply balancing, procurement plans, production allocation, inventory target setting.

* **Operational (0‚Äì12 weeks)**

  * Order confirmation, deployment planning, transport scheduling.

The system is refreshed in **rolling cycles** (weekly or monthly), where the latest data (new demand, updated receipts, production changes) adjusts future plans.

## 5. Managing Imbalances: Understock and Overstock

The biggest challenge in supply chain management is maintaining the balance between **service levels** and **cost efficiency**. Two major risks dominate:

### 5.1 Understock (Service Risk)

Occurs when supply cannot meet demand.
**Common causes:**

* Delayed supplier or transport receipts.
* Demand spikes exceeding forecasts.
* Production disruptions.
* Safety stock set too low.

**Impact:**

* Stockouts at DCs.
* Lost sales and reduced service levels.
* Expedited freight costs.

### 5.2 Overstock (Cost Risk)

Occurs when supply exceeds demand.
**Common causes:**

* Forecast inaccuracies (demand drop).
* Large lot sizes or MOQs.
* Early/duplicate receipts.
* Returns inflow.
* Inventory target or safety stock set too high.

**Impact:**

* High carrying and storage costs.
* Risk of obsolescence.
* Margin erosion through markdowns.

Both conditions are visible through **Projected Stock** analysis:

* **Projected Stock < Safety Stock** ‚Üí Understock alert.
* **Projected Stock > Inventory Target** ‚Üí Overstock alert.

---

## 6. Decision-Making Framework

Planners monitor **alerts and dashboards** to take corrective actions:

* **For Understock:**

  * Expedite inbound shipments.
  * Increase production priority.
  * Redirect inventory from surplus locations.
  * Revise safety stock upwards if recurring.

* **For Overstock:**

  * Reschedule or cancel POs.
  * Postpone production lots.
  * Reallocate inventory to alternative markets.
  * Lower safety stock/inventory target if consistently high.

The decision-making process integrates both **key figure analytics** and **master data constraints** (lead times, lot sizes, MOQs, capacity).

## 7. Integration of Master Data

Beyond transactional key figures, supply chain planning incorporates **master data** that defines the rules of the network:

* **Lead Times** (supplier, production, transportation).
* **Production Constraints** (capacity, yields, batch sizes).
* **Transportation Constraints** (carrier availability, modes, costs).
* **Sourcing Rules** (preferred suppliers, dual sourcing).
* **Service Level Agreements (SLAs)** with customers.

This ensures that planning outputs are **feasible and executable**, not just theoretical.

## 11. Root Cause Framework for Inventory Imbalances

When understock or overstock occurs, it is rarely due to a single factor. Instead, it emerges from **interactions between demand, supply, and planning assumptions**. Root causes can be classified into five broad categories:

1. **Demand Variability**

   * Forecast errors (over-forecast vs. under-forecast).
   * Promotional or seasonal demand surges.
   * Cancellations or sudden drop in orders.

2. **Supply Disruptions**

   * Supplier delays in raw materials.
   * Production breakdowns, capacity shortages.
   * Transport delays or customs clearance issues.

3. **Inventory Policy Misalignment**

   * Safety stock set too high ‚Üí Overstock.
   * Safety stock set too low ‚Üí Understock.
   * Inventory target misaligned with actual service levels.

4. **Planning Constraints**

   * Lot-sizing rules forcing large order quantities.
   * Minimum order quantities (MOQ) creating excess.
   * Lead time assumptions not updated in the system.

5. **Execution Gaps**

   * Open POs not delivered as scheduled.
   * In-transit receipts delayed.
   * Order rescheduling not reflected in the system.

## 12. Multi-Echelon Impact

Imbalances at one node ripple across the network:

* **Supplier Delays ‚Üí Plant Shortages ‚Üí RDC Understock ‚Üí DC Stockouts.**
* **Plant Overproduction ‚Üí RDC Overstock ‚Üí DC Surplus ‚Üí Storage Constraints.**
* **DC Understock ‚Üí Emergency Replenishment ‚Üí Upstream RDC Inventory Drop.**

This emphasizes why explanations must consider **both local and upstream/downstream dynamics**.

## 13. Data Interpretation Rules

Planners use **key figure thresholds** to interpret imbalance situations:

* **Understock**:

  * Projected Stock < Safety Stock ‚Üí At risk.
  * Projected Stock < 0 ‚Üí Definite stockout.
  * Dependent Demand > Available Supply ‚Üí Insufficient coverage.

* **Overstock**:

  * Projected Stock > Inventory Target ‚Üí Surplus.
  * Stock on Hand > Demand Coverage Window (e.g., 12 weeks) ‚Üí Slow-moving stock.
  * Incoming Receipts arriving when demand has reduced.

These rules provide the **analytical backbone** for automated alerts.

## 14. Time Dimension of Planning

Supply chain data is captured in **time buckets** (typically weeks or months):

* Each week, planners generate a **new plan** ‚Üí this becomes the *current week plan*.
* The prior plan shifts into *previous week plan*, allowing comparison.
* This provides context for trend-based reasoning:

  * ‚ÄúWhy did projected stock drop below safety stock this week compared to last week?‚Äù
  * ‚ÄúWhy is excess building over the next 12 weeks?‚Äù

This rolling cycle means **reasons must often reference both current and historical projections**.

## 15. Exception-Based Explanations

Alerts are triggered when exceptions occur:

* **Understock Alert Example:**
  ‚ÄúProjected stock of Product A at DC1000 drops below safety stock in Week 36 due to delayed transport receipts from RDC2000 and higher-than-forecast demand.‚Äù

* **Overstock Alert Example:**
  ‚ÄúProjected stock of Product B at RDC3000 exceeds inventory target by 40% in Weeks 40‚Äì42 due to large lot-size production at Plant P100 and reduced dependent demand from DCs.‚Äù

A robust explanation includes:

1. **Trigger condition** (Projected stock < safety stock / > inventory target).
2. **Root cause factors** (demand, supply, policy, planning, execution).
3. **Propagation impact** (which nodes/customers are affected).

---

## 16. Planner Decision-Making Workflow

To resolve alerts, planners follow structured steps:

* **Understock resolution:**

  * Check if demand was under-forecasted.
  * Verify if supplier or transport delays exist.
  * See if safety stock policy is too low.
  * Propose mitigation (expedite, shift stock, adjust production).

* **Overstock resolution:**

  * Check if demand dropped vs. forecast.
  * Verify if receipts were duplicated or advanced.
  * Review if lot-sizing or MOQs inflated orders.
  * Propose mitigation (reschedule, cancel, redistribute stock).

This workflow allows the LLM to **map key figures ‚Üí detect imbalance ‚Üí reason via framework ‚Üí suggest mitigation.**

## 17. Example End-to-End Flow

Imagine a **plant ‚Üí RDC ‚Üí DC** network for a consumer electronics product:

* **Week 28**: RDC projected stock = 200, safety stock = 150 ‚Üí Healthy.
* **Week 29**: Supplier delays raw material, plant production drops.
* **Week 30**: RDC projected stock falls to 120 (below safety stock).
* **Week 31**: DC dependent demand spikes due to festival season ‚Üí DCs project stockouts.
